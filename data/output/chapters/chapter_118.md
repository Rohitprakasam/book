## Proportional Valves in Hydraulic Systems

Traditional hydraulic systems often rely on on/off valves, sometimes referred to as "bang-bang" valves, which provide only two states: fully open or fully closed. While simple to implement, this type of control introduces significant drawbacks, notably the generation of flow and pressure surges within the hydraulic circuit. These sudden changes in flow and pressure can cause a cascade of issues, including increased noise levels, accelerated wear and tear on hydraulic components, reduced system efficiency, and potential damage to sensitive equipment connected to the hydraulic system. The abrupt start and stop of fluid flow creates pressure spikes that propagate through the system, acting like miniature hydraulic hammers. These pressure spikes can lead to premature failure of seals, hoses, and other components, increasing maintenance costs and downtime. Furthermore, the noise generated by these surges can create an unpleasant and potentially hazardous working environment.

Proportional valves offer a solution to these problems by providing a means to smoothly and gradually control the flow and pressure within a hydraulic system. Unlike on/off valves, proportional valves can be positioned at any point between fully open and fully closed, allowing for precise modulation of fluid flow. This gradual control eliminates the pressure spikes associated with on/off valves, resulting in quieter operation, reduced wear, and improved system performance. Applications that benefit from the use of proportional valves include robotics (where smooth, controlled movements are crucial), machine tools (where precise positioning and cutting forces are required), and aerospace systems (where reliability and performance are paramount). Beyond simply mitigating the issues caused by on/off valves, proportional valves allow the implementation of more advanced control algorithms like PID (Proportional-Integral-Derivative) control, where feedback loops are used to continuously adjust the valve position to achieve the desired system response. Other advanced methodologies include model-predictive control and adaptive control.

## Proportional Valve Actuation: The Role of the DC Solenoid

The heart of many proportional valves is a DC solenoid, which provides the force necessary to precisely position the valve spool. A solenoid is essentially an electromagnet consisting of a coil of wire wrapped around a movable iron core, called the armature. When an electric current flows through the coil, it generates a magnetic field that attracts the armature, causing it to move. The force exerted by the armature is proportional to the current flowing through the coil (although, as we'll see, this relationship is not perfectly linear).

The physics behind this operation is governed by the principles of electromagnetism. The magnetic field strength ($B$) inside the solenoid is directly proportional to the current ($I$) flowing through the coil, the number of turns ($N$) of wire in the coil, and inversely proportional to the length ($l$) of the solenoid. This relationship is expressed by the equation:

$B = \mu_0 \frac{N}{l} I$

where $\mu_0$ is the permeability of free space (approximately $4\pi \times 10^{-7} \, \text{T}\cdot\text{m/A}$).

The force ($F$) exerted on the armature is proportional to the square of the magnetic field strength ($B^2$) and the effective area ($A$) of the armature exposed to the magnetic field. This force can be expressed as:

$F = \frac{B^2 A}{2\mu_0}$

Combining these two equations, we can relate the force ($F$) on the armature directly to the current ($I$) flowing through the solenoid coil:

$F = \frac{\mu_0 A N^2}{2 l^2} I^2$

This equation reveals a non-linear relationship between the force and the current (the force is proportional to the square of the current). For many applications, a linear relationship between current and force is desirable. To achieve this, the solenoid is often pre-biased with a spring or permanent magnet, operating the solenoid within a smaller, more linear portion of its force-current curve. This also helps to compensate for hysteresis, which is the tendency of the armature to lag behind changes in the current due to magnetic domain alignment within the core material. The solenoid's construction typically involves a carefully designed magnetic circuit to maximize the magnetic flux density in the air gap between the coil and the armature, while minimizing leakage flux.

## Control of Proportional Valves: Current vs. Voltage

While it might seem intuitive to control a proportional valve by varying the voltage applied to the solenoid, this approach has significant drawbacks. The electrical control of proportional valves typically relies on a variable current rather than a variable voltage to mitigate the effect of temperature changes on the solenoid coil's resistance.

According to Ohm's Law, the relationship between voltage ($V$), current ($I$), and resistance ($R$) is:

$V = IR$

The resistance of the solenoid coil is not constant; it varies with temperature. The temperature dependence of resistance can be approximated by the following equation:

$R = R_0 (1 + \alpha (T - T_0))$

where $R_0$ is the resistance at a reference temperature $T_0$, $T$ is the current temperature, and $\alpha$ is the temperature coefficient of resistance (typically around 0.004/Â°C for copper).

If a voltage control system is used, any change in coil resistance due to a temperature change will result in a change in current, even if the voltage remains fixed. This change in current directly affects the force generated by the solenoid, leading to inconsistent valve performance. Using the previously derived equation for force as a function of current ($F = \frac{\mu_0 A N^2}{2 l^2} I^2$), we can see that a change in current, $\Delta I$, will cause a change in force, $\Delta F$:

$\Delta F = \frac{\mu_0 A N^2}{l^2} I \Delta I$ (approximating the change as if the force-current relationship were linear).

Therefore, maintaining a constant voltage does NOT guarantee a constant force, and this is unacceptable for many proportional valve applications.

To overcome this problem, a current control system is employed. In a current control system, the current flowing through the solenoid coil is actively regulated, typically using a feedback loop. The current is measured, and the voltage applied to the coil is adjusted to maintain the desired current level, regardless of temperature changes or other variations in coil resistance. A typical implementation might include an operational amplifier (op-amp) and a feedback resistor to precisely control the current.

## Principle of Proportional Pressure Reducing Valve

A proportional pressure reducing valve is a crucial component in hydraulic systems requiring precise pressure control. When the solenoid is energized, it moves the spool to the right, opening the control orifice A and allowing fluid to flow from the supply pressure ($p_1$) to the output port X. Simultaneously, as orifice A opens, orifice B begins to close. The pressure at the control output X ($p_X$) is determined by the relative openings of control orifices A and B. The valve acts as a variable pressure divider, with the pressure drop across each orifice dependent on the flow rate and the orifice area.

The flow rate ($Q$) through an orifice can be described by the following equation:

$Q = C_d A \sqrt{\frac{2(p_{upstream} - p_{downstream})}{\rho}}$

where $C_d$ is the discharge coefficient (typically between 0.6 and 0.8), $A$ is the orifice area, $p_{upstream}$ and $p_{downstream}$ are the upstream and downstream pressures, and $\rho$ is the fluid density.

The spool position is determined by a force balance. The pressure at the output port X ($p_X$) acting on the spool area ($a$) is balanced by the force ($F_{solenoid}$) exerted by the solenoid:

$p_X a = F_{solenoid}$

Combining the solenoid force equation with the force balance on the spool allows us to relate the solenoid current to the output pressure. As the solenoid current increases, the force on the spool increases, causing it to move further to the right. This further opens orifice A and closes orifice B, increasing the output pressure ($p_X$).

It's important to note that there is always a flow to the tank from this type of valve if the output pressure ($p_X$) is less than the supply pressure ($p_1$). This flow is necessary to maintain the desired pressure at the output port. Therefore, it is essential that there is no back pressure in the tank line for the valve to function correctly. Back pressure would interfere with the valve's ability to regulate the output pressure accurately. The ratio of output pressure change to input current change is often referred to as the "pressure gain" of the valve.

## Proportional Directional Control Valve

Proportional directional control valves are used to control the direction and flow rate of hydraulic fluid in a proportional manner. They combine the functionality of directional control valves with the precision of proportional control. These valves use the pressure output from proportional pressure reducing valves to precisely position the main valve spool.

When solenoid 1 is energized, it causes pressure to be applied to pilot port X, moving the main spool to the right against the control spring. The movement of the spool is proportional to the pressure applied to pilot port X, which in turn is proportional to the current in solenoid 1. The control spring provides a restoring force that opposes the force exerted by the pilot pressure, ensuring that the spool returns to its center position when the solenoid is de-energized. The spring constant determines the sensitivity of the valve: a stiffer spring requires more pilot pressure to achieve the same spool displacement.

The main spool lands are notched, which allows for a gradual opening of the flow paths as the spool moves. As the spool moves to the right, it progressively opens the flow paths from P to B and A to T (where P is the pressure port, B and A are the work ports, and T is the tank port). This progressive opening provides fine control over the flow rate to the actuator.

The force balance on the main spool can be expressed as:

$p_X a = kx$

where $p_X$ is the pilot pressure, $a$ is the spool area, $k$ is the spring constant, and $x$ is the spool displacement.

The spool displacement is directly related to the flow rates through the valve ports. While the exact relationship depends on the valve geometry (notch shape and size), it can be approximated as a linear relationship for simplicity:

$Q \approx K_v x$

where $Q$ is the flow rate and $K_v$ is a valve coefficient.

De-energizing solenoid 1 will depressurize spring chamber C, and the control spring will centralize the spool, closing off the flow paths. The valve's performance is also affected by the spool overlap or underlap. Overlap refers to the condition where the spool lands are wider than the ports, resulting in a deadband where small spool movements do not affect the flow. Underlap refers to the condition where the spool lands are narrower than the ports, resulting in leakage even when the spool is in the centered position.
