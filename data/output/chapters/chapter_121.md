Hydraulic systems often incorporate interlocks and alternative control positions to enhance safety, prevent unintended operation, and provide operational flexibility. Interlocks are safety mechanisms designed to prevent specific actions from occurring unless certain prerequisite conditions are met. They act as safeguards, ensuring that operations occur in the correct sequence and under safe circumstances, thus minimizing the risk of accidents or damage to equipment. Think of interlocks as a series of checks and balances within the hydraulic circuit, preventing actions that could be harmful if performed out of order or under the wrong conditions. These interlocks are often implemented using hydraulic logic elements that mimic the behavior of electrical logic gates like AND, OR and NOT.

Alternative control positions offer redundancy and flexibility in controlling the hydraulic system. These alternative positions allow for continued operation even if the primary control system fails or becomes unavailable. For instance, a system might have a manual override that can be engaged if the automated control system malfunctions. This ensures that critical functions can still be performed, even in emergency situations or during maintenance. Alternative control positions provide a backup, enhancing the system's reliability and availability. Alternative control can also refer to the ability to control a hydraulic actuator from multiple locations.

Historically, the implementation of interlocks and alternative control positions has evolved alongside the development of hydraulic technology. Early hydraulic systems relied on manual interlocks and simple mechanical linkages. As hydraulic systems became more complex, sophisticated hydraulic and electrohydraulic interlocks were developed, offering improved reliability and precision. Today, many hydraulic systems utilize programmable logic controllers (PLCs) to manage interlocks and control functions, providing even greater flexibility and control.

In industrial applications, interlocks are crucial in presses, heavy machinery, and other equipment where safety is paramount. For example, a hydraulic press might use a two-hand control system as an interlock, requiring the operator to use both hands to activate the press, thus preventing them from placing their hands in the danger zone. Alternative control positions are commonly found in aircraft hydraulic systems, where multiple control systems are in place to ensure continued operation in the event of a failure. They are also common in large construction equipment allowing operators to perform maintenance or override automated features.
